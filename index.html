Eyes in the sky

China's Rapidly Expanding Space-based Spy Program

  • view the story

China's spy satellite program

has been rapidly expanding in recent years, rivaling that of the United States. The United States operates more than twice as many spy satellites than China. But more than half of China’s 23 active spy satellites were launched between 2011 and 2014, while the majority of United States spy satellites were launched before 2009. In 2014 alone, China launched seven of what western military experts believe are spy satellites.

Number of spy satellites by country


The United States and China lead the world with reconnaissance -- or spy -- satellites, or those that are used to collect intelligence information on military and other activities of foreign countries. Together, the two countries account for 75 percent of the spy satellites currently in orbit.

According to Joan Johnson-Freese, a National Securities Affairs professor at the U.S. Naval War College, China “has been expanding its military space capabilities to include most of the capabilities already possessed by the United States, including reconnaissance.”

China alone operates two and a half times more spy satellites than all European nations combined. As more and more satellites are launched, it becomes a literal battle for space -- with China on the offense.

“In short, China has come to the same realization as the U.S. and Russia that space capabilities are crucial to be a regional or global power,” said Brian Weeden, technical advisor at Secure World Foundation, an organization with a mission to promote cooperative solutions for sustainability in space. “I think the driver is the same as it is for many other countries - a growing need for intelligence data to support national decision-making and also warfighting.”

China has regularly launched satellites in pairs or triplets in recent years, with functionalities ranging from broad, real-time video Earth imaging to an Automatic Identification System for relaying specific ship location information. Satellite and other space launches must be reported to the United Nations Registry of Space Objects by spacefaring nations, but inaccurate or otherwise incomplete data -- like vague purpose definitions -- can raise many questions. Reconnaissance satellites, for example, could be listed as communications or Earth observation satellites. Many satellites also serve multiple purposes while in orbit, and any movement or change risks wrong interpretations.

“The danger of dual use technology is that a security dilemma can be created where countries take actions to technologically keep up or go ahead of other countries, even though those actions may not be in its long-term interests. Subsequently, chances of crisis escalation increase while trust among countries decrease,” said Johnson-Freese.

As China increases its capabilities, the United States feels compelled to escalate and expand its counterspace, or offensive-defensive, space technology, said Johnson-Freese, which has already been evidenced in the United States’ military space budget and plans for coming years. In a 2013 annual report to Congress on military and security developments involving the People’s Republic of China, the U.S. Department of Defense said that the Pentagon has been monitoring China’s space activities, which show no sign of slowing. NASA and the Department of Defense did not respond to requests for comment.

But, one thing is certain: the space in space is filling up, and quickly.
